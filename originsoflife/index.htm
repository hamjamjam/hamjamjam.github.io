Origins of Life

Tools used: Rhino, Processing, Rhino, 3D printing, Wax casting

Fall 2013

Origins of life is a highly abstract examination of the formation of cell membranes, dictated by physical forces on phosopholipids thus treating them as the building blocks of this system of "life". This was my first studio at MIT.



The origins of life and definition of what is "alive" is based largely on how each individual defines his own parameters for the concept of life and upon the system in which one is setting these parameters. In the physical system, or the "real world", as it were, the ingredients of the most basic life form are the four elements; Carbon, Hydrogen, Nitrogen and Oxygen operating together to form a system that carries out certain processes.





It is widely accepted that single cellular life forms existed on our planet before any other. This is due to their (comparatively) simple make up and structure which is thought to have built up to the life forms that we know today. Indeed, cells are thus the buildling blocks of life.



Abstracting from the idea of a membrane, we look now into the patterns produced as phosopholipid molecules are packed together and thus arrive at the basis of this project.



The project starts by writing a code (in Processing) to simulate the movement of particles within a cell membrane and then further abstracting the process to a 3D model (pictured). The model was then 3D printed and cast in wax in order to examine the behavior of the system in response to heat -- finally we arrive at watching the system instead of defining it as we had done in the first part of the project.
